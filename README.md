## Introduction:

Shark attacks are a global safety concern that affects coastal tourism, marine sport industries, and public health policy. Despite growing interest in ocean activities over the past century, there has been limited data-driven insight into when, where, and why shark attacks occur most frequently.

This project analyzes a comprehensive dataset spanning over 100 years of reported shark attacks worldwide, covering victim demographics, attack triggers, shark species, geographic locations, and time-of-day patterns.

### Analysis:

### Shark Attack Trends Over Time
Looking at annual attacks from 1900 to the present, the direction is unmistakably upward. The climb becomes noticeably steeper after 1950 and peaks in the 2000s and 2010s. What this tells us is that the growth in attacks tracks closely with the growth in coastal tourism and ocean recreation, not with any shift in shark behavior or population size. People are simply spending more time in the water than ever before.

### Most Dangerous Countries
The United States stands well ahead of every other country with 2,042 recorded attacks, nearly double Australia’s 1,147. South Africa sits in third place at 541. While the US leads in total volume, Australia’s fatal attack count tells a different story. Despite fewer total attacks, Australia records 264 fatal cases compared to the US at 202, pointing to the presence of more lethal species in Australian waters rather than a higher frequency of encounters.

### Most Injured Body Parts
Legs take the most damage by a significant margin at 1,363 cases, followed by feet at 745, arms at 422, and hands and fingers at 372. This makes sense when you think about how people move through the water. Whether swimming, surfing, or wading, the lower body is almost always the closest point to a shark approaching from below, and the most visible one too.

### Time-of-Day Patterns
Afternoons are by far the most dangerous time of day, accounting for 1,271 attacks. That lines up with when beaches are busiest and ocean activity peaks. What is worth paying closer attention to, though, is what happens in the evening and at night. Attack numbers drop, but fatality rates go up. Slower emergency response times and reduced visibility for rescuers mean that when attacks do happen after dark, they are more likely to be fatal.

### Attack Triggers
The overwhelming majority of attacks, 3,864 in total, were unprovoked. In most of these cases, the person had no prior interaction with the shark at all. They simply entered the water. Provoked attacks account for 515 cases and typically involve deliberate contact such as fishing or handling a shark. The data makes it clear that in the vast majority of incidents, humans entered shark territory and the shark responded.

### Species Analysis
White Sharks record the highest attack volume across the dataset and carry a fatality rate of around 20 to 25 percent. Tiger Sharks come in second and, while their total attack count is lower, their fatality rate is notably higher, making them statistically the deadliest species per encounter. Bronze Whalers rank third in total attacks and carry a meaningful fatality rate. Bull Sharks round out the top four and, despite the lowest volume among the group, still represent a consistent threat across the records.

### Additional Insight — Gender and Age Profile
Male victims outnumber female victims across every single age group in the dataset. The Youth category, covering roughly ages 18 to 30, sees the highest concentration of attacks overall. This is the demographic most likely to surf, dive, and swim in open water, often in conditions and locations that carry higher risk. The Child age group is smaller in absolute numbers, but that lower count should not reduce concern. Younger victims are more physically vulnerable, and the consequences of an attack at that age tend to be more severe.

## Conclusion and Recommendations:

More than a hundred years of data tells a clear story: shark attacks are not increasing because sharks are becoming more aggressive. They are increasing because more people are spending more time in the ocean. The pattern is consistent across the dataset. Most attacks are unprovoked, they happen most often in the afternoon, and the typical victim is a young male. The United States and Australia carry the highest attack counts, and four species, White Sharks, Tiger Sharks, Bronze Whalers, and Bull Sharks, appear most frequently across the records.

Afternoon hours between 12pm and 6pm represent the clearest window for preventive action, and coastal authorities should treat that time block with structured beach advisories and visible safety measures. Pairing that with real-time detection tools like surveillance drones and sonar buoys would give beach management teams the ability to respond before incidents occur rather than after. 

Young male surfers and swimmers are the group that shows up most in this data, so awareness campaigns need to speak directly to that audience in the spaces they actually pay attention to. 

Evening hours may see fewer attacks overall, but the fatality rates are higher, which means lifeguard coverage cannot drop off when the afternoon crowd thins out. 

At a broader level, the inconsistency in how attacks are recorded across different countries makes it harder to draw reliable global conclusions. A shared international reporting standard would strengthen the data and make future research far more useful for policymakers and safety agencies alike.
