## Introduction:

Shark attacks are a global safety concern that affects coastal tourism, marine sport industries, and public health policy. Despite growing interest in ocean activities over the past century, there has been limited data-driven insight into when, where, and why shark attacks occur most frequently.

This project analyzes a comprehensive dataset spanning over 100 years of reported shark attacks worldwide, covering victim demographics, attack triggers, shark species, geographic locations, and time-of-day patterns.

### Analysis:

### Shark Attack Trends Over Time
A line chart of annual attacks from 1900 to present reveals a clear upward trend, particularly accelerating after 1950. The peak attack years cluster in the 2000s–2010s, suggesting modern coastal activity levels as the primary driver — not necessarily shark population increases.

### Most Dangerous Countries
The United States dominates with 2,042 total attacks, nearly double second-place Australia (1,147). South Africa ranks third with 541 attacks. The US and Australia also lead in fatal attacks at 202 and 264 respectively, though Australia’s higher fatality ratio reflects the presence of more lethal species in its waters.

### Most Injured Body Parts
Leg injuries account for 1,363 cases which is the highest of any body part, followed by Foot (745), Arm (422), and Hand/Finger (372). This pattern is consistent with lower-body exposure during swimming, surfing, and wading activities, where legs and feet are closest to and most visible from below.

### Time-of-Day Patterns
Afternoon is overwhelmingly the most dangerous period, accounting for 1,271 attacks. This aligns with peak beach and ocean activity hours. While absolute attack numbers drop at night, fatality rates increase during Evening and Night hours, likely due to slower emergency response and reduced visibility for rescuers.

### Attack Triggers
The majority of attacks (3,864) were unprovoked, confirming that in most cases, humans entered shark territory without deliberately engaging the animal. Provoked attacks (515) represent deliberate human-shark interaction (fishing, handling).

### Species Analysis
White Sharks lead in total attacks with the highest volume, while maintaining a fatality rate around 20–25%. Tiger Sharks follow in second with a notably higher fatality rate, making them the deadliest species per attack. 

### Additional Insight — Gender & Age Profile
Male victims overwhelmingly outnumber female victims across all age groups, with the Youth category (ages ~18–30) representing the highest attack concentration. This reflects the demographic most likely to engage in high-risk ocean activities such as surfing, diving, and swimming in open water. The Child age group, despite lower absolute numbers, carries a disproportionate concern given vulnerability.

## Conclusion and Recommendations:

Over a century of data confirms that shark attack frequency is rising due to increased coastal human activity, not changes in shark behavior. Unprovoked attacks dominate, afternoon hours are the highest-risk period, and young male victims account for the majority of recorded cases. The United States and Australia lead in total attacks, with White Sharks, Tiger Sharks, Bronze Whalers, and Bull Sharks as the most implicated species.

Coastal authorities should enforce afternoon beach advisories between 12pm and 6pm and invest in real-time shark detection technology such as surveillance drones and sonar buoys. Awareness campaigns should target young male ocean users, the demographic most represented in the data. Night lifeguard coverage should be strengthened given the higher fatality rates recorded during those hours. Internationally, a standardized attack reporting framework is needed to improve data quality and support long-term public safety research.
